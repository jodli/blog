title: The Modernization That Never Lands
date: 2026-03-30
draft: false
---

I work on a legacy product. It's big, it's old, it makes real money. I've been here for seven years now — and for as long as I can remember, someone has been trying to modernize it. It started before I even joined.

We didn't abandon any of these efforts. We committed. We built new architectures, new components, new platforms. We stuck with them. That's what makes it so strange — we did the work, and we're still not there.

## The limbo

The details change every time. Sometimes it's a new frontend framework. Sometimes it's a backend rearchitecture. Sometimes it's a platform that's supposed to make everything modular and reusable. Each effort had a real plan, real people, real commitment. And each time, the new thing got built. It works. Parts of it are in production.

But the old thing is still there too. We can't just switch over — there are customers relying on the existing behavior, compatibility requirements, features that haven't been re-implemented yet. So we end up maintaining both. Building new features on the new architecture while keeping the old one alive. Every change has to work with both worlds.

We're somewhere around halfway through re-implementing the existing features in the new components. We're modernizing the design along the way, rethinking how things should work, not just copying what was there before. That sounds like progress — and it is. But halfway means the other half is still the old system.

And the finish line keeps moving. Not because anyone is doing anything wrong, but because the product doesn't stop while you modernize it. New requirements come in. The market shifts. The assumptions we started with — who it's for, what matters, what "modern" even means — those don't stay fixed for years. By the time we're halfway, we're modernizing toward something that no longer matches where the world has moved.

## Right now

For a long time there's been this feeling of being "almost done." It never quite resolves. And recently, the conversation shifted again. Not about the technical approach — about the direction itself. Where exactly we're heading, and whether it's still the right place.

Nobody's incompetent. Everyone involved is smart, experienced, and genuinely trying to do the right thing. That's what makes it so frustrating.

## I don't have a conclusion

I keep thinking there's something here, but I'm not sure I can articulate it yet. Something about how modernization becomes a permanent state instead of a transition. Something about the weight of maintaining two worlds at once. Something about how the hardest part of building software isn't the building.

I don't know how this ends. It's still in motion. I'll keep watching.
